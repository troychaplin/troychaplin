## The concept

A WordPress block theme for a frontend developer's blog and portfolio, built around the metaphor of a working letterpress shop. Posts are broadsides. The archive is the type drawer. The site speaks the language of a craft where typography and code are siblings — both about precision in symbols.

For eventual WordPress directory submission, so the metaphor should be strong enough to be memorable but loose enough that any thoughtful writer's content could live in it.

## Where the weirdness lives

**Typography as architecture.** Kerning marks, registration crosses, leading rules, type-specimen layouts. That's the loud channel.

Color, motion, and ornament stay quiet so the type can do the work. One loud channel, three quiet ones.

## Anchors, not a checklist

- "Broadside" or "impression" as a name for posts — or something better you find
- Type-specimen-style headers somewhere
- Registration marks, fleurons, or printer's flowers used as dividers and flourishes
- An archive view that reads like a type drawer or specimen book
- A palette built from ink and paper — but make a real choice, don't default to black on white

## What I want from you first

Before any code:

1. **Pick an era.** 18th-century broadside? Victorian poster shop? Mid-century specimen book? Contemporary risograph studio? It changes everything — letterforms, color, density.
2. **Typographic direction**, with a reason for the pairing — and remember type *is* the personality here.
3. **A named palette** — colors named in the language of ink and paper.
4. **Three or four block patterns** that couldn't exist in any other theme.
5. **A first sketch of the homepage.**

Show me thinking before code. I want to react to direction before we commit.

## Constraints

- WordPress block theme, theme.json driven
- Directory-submission ready (GPL-compatible fonts, accessibility, no proprietary anything)
- Light and dark modes both — print shops have day shifts and night shifts
- The metaphor never breaks character, but it never winks at the camera either

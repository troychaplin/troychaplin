## The concept

A WordPress block theme for a frontend developer's blog and portfolio, built around the metaphor of a shortwave radio operator's logbook. Posts are transmissions. Archives are logged sessions. The site borrows the vocabulary of operators — frequencies, callsigns, signal reports, propagation conditions — applied to the rhythms of writing and shipping work.

For eventual WordPress directory submission, so the metaphor should be strong enough to be memorable but loose enough that someone else's content could live in it.

## Where the weirdness lives

**Operator metadata as chrome.** Callsigns, frequencies, signal-strength bars, transmission timestamps, propagation indicators. That's the loud channel.

Typography, color, and motion stay quieter so the operator's instruments can sing.

## Anchors, not a checklist

- "Transmission" or "broadcast" as a name for posts
- Each post tagged with a callsign-or-frequency-style identifier
- Signal-strength dots or bars somewhere as visual rhythm
- Dial typography for headers — numerals matter
- An archive that reads like a station logbook
- A palette suggesting consoles and dim rooms — amber-on-black, console green, or something better you find

## What I want from you first

Before any code:

1. **Pick a station.** 1940s ham shack? Cold War listening post? Antarctic relay? Modern DXer's home setup? Pirate radio? It changes everything.
2. **Typographic direction**, with a reason — numerals and monospace earn their place here, but don't be lazy.
3. **A named palette** — colors named in the language of stations and signals.
4. **Three or four block patterns** that couldn't exist in any other theme.
5. **A first sketch of the homepage.**

Show me thinking before code. I want to react to direction before we commit.

## Constraints

- WordPress block theme, theme.json driven
- Directory-submission ready (GPL-compatible fonts, accessibility, no proprietary anything)
- Light and dark modes both — but consider which is the truer default; radio rooms are typically dim
- The metaphor never breaks character, but it never winks at the camera either
